Affordable Housing

Why Affordable Housing Is the Most Important Investment a City Can Make

📅 June 2025
✎ Chinedum Ndukwe
⌚ 6 min read

Cities that neglect affordable housing don't just fail their lowest-income residents — they undermine the economic engine that makes urban growth possible in the first place. Chinedum Ndukwe has spent over a decade in Cincinnati making the case, in boardrooms and at planning tables, that accessible housing is infrastructure just as surely as roads and water lines.

When most people think about infrastructure, they picture bridges, transit systems, utility networks. These are the physical systems a city cannot function without. But Chinedum Ndukwe, founder of Kingsley and Company in Cincinnati, Ohio, argues that we've been thinking about infrastructure too narrowly. Housing — specifically, affordable housing — is the foundation on which every other urban system rests. When it fails, everything else does too.

The Hidden Cost of Housing Instability

The case for affordable housing is sometimes framed as a moral argument, a matter of compassion for those in need. That framing isn't wrong, but it's incomplete. The economic case is just as compelling. When workers can't afford to live near their jobs, cities suffer labor shortages in the very sectors — healthcare, education, service industries — that keep daily life running. When families are housing-insecure, children perform worse in school, healthcare utilization rises, and civic participation drops. Every cost that gets externalized from the housing market lands somewhere else in the municipal budget.

Ndukwe saw this cycle up close in Cincinnati. The city, like many mid-sized American metros, had significant housing stock that sat underutilized while demand for affordable units far outpaced supply. The math was straightforward: without intervention, the gap would only widen. His firm, Kingsley and Company, was built in part to close that gap — not through charity, but through development that made economic and social sense simultaneously.

"The best housing projects don't just put roofs over heads. They create the conditions under which families can build wealth, children can focus on learning, and neighborhoods can attract the investment they deserve."

What Victory Vistas Demonstrated

The Victory Vistas project is one of Kingsley and Company's clearest demonstrations of this philosophy in action. When the development was completed, the firm had secured 11 housing vouchers for low-income individuals — a tangible result that translated directly into stable housing for families who would otherwise have remained on waiting lists. But beyond the vouchers, the project contributed to the revitalization of a neighborhood that had been underinvested for years.

The choice to pursue vouchers as part of the project wasn't purely altruistic. It reflected a calculated view that mixed-income developments perform better over the long term. They attract more diverse tenants, benefit from more stable occupancy rates, and create social environments that reduce the concentrated disadvantage associated with segregated low-income housing. The lessons from Victory Vistas continue to inform how Ndukwe approaches every new development Kingsley and Company considers.

The Developer's Responsibility

There is a temptation in real estate development to treat affordable housing as someone else's problem — a government mandate, a nonprofit mission, a policy concern. Ndukwe rejects that framing entirely. Private developers, he argues, have both the capacity and the obligation to participate in solutions. The financing tools exist. The demand is unmistakable. What's often missing is the will to do the harder, slower work of community engagement, navigating housing voucher programs, and accepting slightly thinner margins in exchange for deeper impact.

That's a trade Chinedum is willing to make. His work on community-centered development practices reflects a conviction that the most successful developers of the next generation will be those who understand how to build trust with communities, not just close transactions within them. In Cincinnati's competitive development market, that reputation has become one of Kingsley and Company's most durable assets.

What Cincinnati Can Teach Other Cities

Cincinnati occupies an interesting position in the national housing conversation. It's a city with genuine affordability challenges but also a city that has shown a willingness to innovate — through inclusionary zoning pilots, tax increment financing structures, and partnerships between private developers, the city, and nonprofit housing organizations. Ndukwe's civic roles, including his service on the Mayor's task force, have placed him inside those conversations.

What he's seen is that the cities making progress aren't waiting for a silver-bullet federal program. They're building coalition by coalition, project by project, neighborhood by neighborhood. The Blair and Victory Vistas are part of that story. They won't solve Cincinnati's housing crisis on their own, but they demonstrate that solutions are possible — and that private developers can be genuine partners in achieving them. You can follow ongoing updates about Chinedum Ndukwe's development work and community initiatives via his Issuu publications.

Conclusion

The case for affordable housing investment isn't complicated — cities that house their people well, at every income level, outperform those that don't. What's complicated is doing the work: navigating financing, building political will, designing for longevity, and earning the trust of communities that have seen promises broken before. Chinedum Ndukwe has spent his post-NFL career doing exactly that work in Cincinnati. The projects are the proof.
